Call Etiquette
4 Q
01 How to greet and open a telephonic interview call? 📞 Call ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Answer within 2 rings. Find a quiet place before the scheduled time.
  • Speak clearly, slightly slower than normal — they can't see your face.
  • Always introduce yourself first even before they ask.
  • Keep a glass of water nearby. Have your resume printed or open on screen.
✅ Exact words to use
"Hello, good morning! This is Akhilesh speaking."

[They say: "Hi Akhilesh, this is [Name] from [Company], calling for your interview."]

"Hi [Name], great to connect with you! Yes, I'm all set. Please go ahead."
✏️ Practice notes
02 What if you didn't hear or understand a question clearly on the call? 📞 Call ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Never guess and answer the wrong question — it wastes everyone's time.
  • Politely ask them to repeat. It is completely professional to do this.
  • You can also repeat what you understood and confirm before answering.
✅ What to say
"I'm sorry, the line was a little unclear — could you please repeat that?"

OR

"Just to make sure I understood correctly — you're asking about [restate question]? Yes, so…"
03 How to close and end the telephonic interview politely? 📞 Call ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Thank them by name — it shows you were paying attention.
  • Ask about next steps — shows you're serious about the process.
  • Don't hang up abruptly. Let them end the call first.
✅ Closing words
"Thank you so much, [Name], for taking the time to speak with me today. I really enjoyed learning more about the role. Could you share what the next steps in the process look like? I look forward to hearing from you. Have a great day!"
04 Do you have any questions for us? (End of interview) 📞 Call ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Never say "No, I'm fine." — it signals low interest.
  • Have 2 questions ready. Focus on the role and team, not salary/leave in first round.
  • Good questions show you've done your homework about the company.
✅ 2 strong questions to ask
1. "Could you tell me about the current infrastructure setup and what the biggest DevOps challenges the team is working on in the next 6–12 months?"

2. "What does success look like for someone in this role in the first 3 months?"

📌 Key Tips
  • State your exact notice period clearly — don't be vague.

19 Tell me about a time you made a mistake at work. How did you handle it? 🧠 Behavioural ▼
📌 Use the STAR method
  • Situation: What was the context?
  • Task: What was your responsibility?
  • Action: What did you do (including the mistake and the fix)?
  • Result: What was the outcome and what did you learn?
✅ Sample answer
"Early in my role, I accidentally ran a Terraform apply command targeting the production environment instead of staging. Fortunately, I caught it quickly and used Terraform's state management to roll back the changes before any real damage was done. After that incident, I set up separate state files with mandatory environment variable confirmation, and added a Jenkins approval gate before any infra changes touch production. It was an uncomfortable experience but it made our deployment process significantly safer."
✏️ Your own real example
20 Describe a situation where you had a conflict with a teammate. How did you resolve it? 🧠 Behavioural ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Keep the conflict professional — technical disagreement, not personal drama.
  • Show maturity: you listened, found common ground, and focused on the goal.
  • Don't blame the other person — own your part too.
✅ Sample answer
"There was a situation where a developer and I disagreed on how a deployment should be handled — they wanted a direct FTP approach to keep it simple, while I wanted to go through the Jenkins pipeline for consistency and auditability. We had a direct conversation, I walked them through the risks of bypassing the pipeline — no rollback, no quality gate — and they agreed to try the proper approach. We ended up refining the pipeline together to make it faster so they didn't feel it was slowing them down. We've had zero deployment issues from that service since."
✏️ Your real example

22 We have a counter-offer — if your current company matches our offer, will you still join us? 📄 Offer ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • This is a test of loyalty and seriousness. Most companies don't ask this early but some do.
  • Reassure them that your decision isn't purely money-driven.
  • Don't sound desperate either way — stay calm and confident.
✅ Sample answer
"That's a fair question. If I were leaving only for money, a counter-offer would change things. But honestly, my primary motivation for this move is the scope of the role, the technical environment, and the growth opportunity — which my current company can't replicate just by matching a number. If I get to that point, my decision will be based on the full picture, and based on what I know so far, this opportunity is genuinely where I want to be."

32 If we give you an offer, how long will you take to decide? 📄 Offer ▼
📌 Key Tips
  • Don't say "I need a month" — it signals low interest or that you're shopping for a better offer.
  • 2–3 business days is a professional, reasonable ask. 1 week max if you have other processes running.
  • If you have another offer coming, you can mention it — it creates honest urgency without playing games.
✅ Sample answer
"I like to make decisions thoughtfully, not impulsively — so I'd appreciate 2 to 3 business days to review the offer carefully. If I have any questions about the terms, I'll reach out quickly. I'm not the kind of person who takes weeks to decide — if the role and numbers make sense, I move fast."
❌ Don't say

"I'll let you know whenever I'm ready" — sounds arrogant. Always give a timeline.
